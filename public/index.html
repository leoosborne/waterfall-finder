Waterfall Finder

Where, and also When

There is something wonderous about a waterfall — candy for those of us who love exploring nature — when water, heeding gravity's pull, slowly carves its stories into stone. Among the most magical are the intermittent waterfalls. Most of them hidden deep in the woods, on unnamed watercourses, fed only by a chance rainstorm, bursting into life for a short time, then quickly draining the hillside and leaving only ancient trails worn into rock as evidence.

To expereince these fleeting wonders, one must not only know where to find them, but when to go looking for them.

Waterfall Finder takes the guesswork out of chasing waterfalls. We share the secrets of where and when in hopes you will choose to seek out some of these lesser seen waterfalls in their delicate, fleeting brilliance. We believe that if more people expereince these special places, the better our chances are of protecting them.

Mammoth Cave National Park

Mammoth Cave is a national treasure. Located near Brownsville, KY, Mammoth Cave has a long history of exploration and use by humans, dating back to prehistory when people used it for shelter and collecting crystals and salts. During the late 1700s, the cave was "resdiscovered" as a source of saltpeter, which was mined to make gunpowder for the War of 1812. In 1816, it quickly gained the attention of the nacent America tourisim industry as a privately owned attraction that saw millions of visitors long before it became a national park, while at the same time serving as a hospital for tuberculosis patients. The cave system began the transition from private to public ownership in 1926 and attained National Park status in 1941.

Above Ground at Mammoth Cave

The formation of Mammoth Cave started over 330 million years ago when sediments were deposited in a shallow inland sea and later hardened to become limestone and sandstone. The green river and its surrounding stream valley eventually carved underground streams through the softer limestone while leaving the harder sandstone layers in tact. This same process is continues today, both below the ground to form the cave system, and also above ground the sculpt waterfalls, rock houses, sink holes and other karst topography formations.

The Park has many hiking trails that lead to sink holes, creek beds, overlooks and camping sites. The trails are a great way to get started in exploring the area, but to find the intermittent waterfalls one must leave the ease of the trail and follow the creeks down into the valleys. This is where to find the hidden waterfalls. With a little advanced research and preparation, you will be able to see these falls when they are flowing.

To be safe, comfortable and happy during your adventure, you should first learn the basics of navigating by map and compass (GPS and internet are not always reliable in wild places), know how to safely hike off-trail ("bushwhacking"), how to protect the native flora and fauna, how to leave no trace of your passage, how to pack the essentials of food, water and clothing, how to be prepared to treat minor injuries and to always let someone know where you are going and when you expect to return. Knowing these things is essential to responsibly visiting the backcountry, and easy to learn from books or by hiking with friends who have already acquired the necessary skills.
